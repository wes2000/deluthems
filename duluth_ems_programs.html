How To Read This

One certification, two stories.

In Minnesota and Wisconsin, EMT-Basic is a short, single-semester gateway. The Paramedic credential — a different animal entirely — is a 16-to-20 month commitment that requires you to already hold an EMT license before you start. No school in this guide accepts you straight into Paramedic without the EMT step first. Plan accordingly.

01

Lake Superior College

2101 Trinity Rd · Duluth · ERTC, Gary New Duluth · (218) 733-7601
EMT-Basic No Paramedic

The most prominent EMT-Basic option in Duluth itself. Lake Superior College runs a single 9-credit certificate (FIRE 2486) at its Emergency Response Training Center in Gary New Duluth — a facility shared with firefighting and rescue training, complete with live-fire and accident-staging props. Students often train alongside fire and public-safety crews.

On-campusLectures & skills at ERTC; clinical ride-alongs separate

Who it's for & what's required

  • Must be at least 16 years old to take the course
  • Must be at least 18 and felony-free to earn national certification
  • Current NetStudy background check required to participate in clinicals
  • No prior EMS experience needed — this is the entry point
  • Apply through LSC general admissions; this is a "Program with Special Entry Requirements"

Typical weekly load

EMT-Basic at colleges like LSC typically runs 9–12 contact hours per week across two or three evenings (lecture + lab), plus required outside-of-class clinical ride-along hours scheduled with affiliated ambulance services. Plan for an additional 6–10 hours of study weekly.

Why pick LSC

  • In Duluth proper — no commute to Superior or Hibbing
  • Cheap — among the lowest tuition in the Minnesota State system
  • Realistic facility — ERTC is regionally known for first-responder training
  • Stackable into LSC's Fire Medic and Fire Technology AAS pathways
Limitation — LSC does not currently offer a Paramedic program. After completing the EMT certificate at LSC, you'd transfer to Mayo Clinic (Duluth satellite) or Northwood Tech (Superior) for medic training.
02

Mayo Clinic School of Health Sciences — Paramedic

Duluth Satellite Site · Hosted at the main MCAS ambulance station, Duluth
No EMT-Basic Paramedic

This is the headline Paramedic option in town. Mayo Clinic's Emergency Medicine Paramedic Program is a 20-month program coordinated out of Rochester, with a fully staffed Duluth satellite site. A Duluth-specific instructor runs lectures and skills in-person at the local ambulance station, while the Rochester cohort is brought in virtually for shared lectures.

Prerequisites — every one is mandatory

  • High school diploma or GED
  • At least 18 years old by program start
  • Current NREMT EMT-Basic certification (you must already be a certified EMT)
  • Current AHA Basic Life Support (BLS) provider card
  • Current valid driver's license
  • U.S. citizen or permanent resident (Mayo does not sponsor visas for this program)
  • One college-level course completed before the program — Mayo recommends HCOP 1610: Medical Terminology at RCTC

Application materials

  • CV / résumé
  • AHA BLS card, NREMT EMT card, MN EMT (EMSRB) provider card
  • 500-word essay on motivation for paramedicine
  • Two recommendations — one general (teacher, supervisor), one professional (preferably your EMS supervisor)
  • Unofficial transcripts at application; official transcripts upon admission

What "Duluth site" actually means

Most clinical and field shifts (ambulance ride time) happen in Duluth or surrounding areas. However, cadaver labs, AHA courses, and select specialty clinicals require travel to Rochester. Plan and budget for that. Duluth students who choose the A.S. degree track must additionally enroll at Rochester Community & Technical College for online general-education courses.

Typical weekly load

Mayo describes its weekly schedule as variable — 8- and 12-hour days, with the days per week shifting based on whether the week is dominated by classroom lecture, simulation lab, hospital clinical, or ambulance field internship. Expect this to function essentially as a full-time educational commitment; outside employment is permitted only if it does not conflict with program responsibilities. In total, students cover a 40-credit didactic load plus extensive clinical and field internship hours over 20 months.

Apply & learn more Online application opens annually January 1. Direct contact through Mayo Clinic's admissions inquiry form, or through the program overview page linked above. Note the program is approved by the Minnesota EMSRB for paramedic instruction.

Mayo Clinic Ambulance Service — EMT-Basic

Duluth station · Direct ambulance-service training · AMBSVCEducation@mayo.edu · 507-538-7159
EMT-Basic No Paramedic (see #02)

Distinct from Mayo's Paramedic program — same parent organization, different pathway. Mayo Clinic Ambulance Service is licensed by the Minnesota EMSRB as an EMS training institution and runs initial EMT-Basic, EMT Refresher, and Emergency Medical Responder courses. It also hosts the EMT-in-Training Program — a paid, accelerated track piloted in Austin, MN in 2024 with plans to expand to other MCAS service areas (which include Duluth).

Two ways in

  • Traditional EMT-Basic course — open enrollment, runs at MCAS training centers including Duluth. Contact AMBSVCEducation@mayo.edu for current schedules and tuition. Standard MN EMT-Basic course length is 130–160 hours over a semester.
  • EMT-in-Training Program — Mayo's paid pipeline for career-changers with no prior EMS background. Trainees ride with a paramedic-EMT crew from week one (mostly observing), are CPR-certified and N-95 fit-tested up front, then move toward full EMT certification over ten weeks. Upon completion, graduates are eligible to apply for full-time EMT positions with Mayo Clinic Ambulance Service.

Typical weekly load — EMT-in-Training

Five days per week, with daily shifts in the 8–9 hour range. This is a full-time, paid commitment — not a side-of-the-desk course. Trainees alternate between classroom learning and ambulance ride-alongs as their skills accumulate.

Why this matters — for Duluth-area career-changers without college aspirations, this is arguably the most accessible path: no tuition, immediate paycheck, and a guaranteed track to a Mayo employment interview. Availability of the EMT-in-Training cohort in Duluth specifically depends on local recruitment needs — check directly.

600 N 21st St · Superior, WI 54880 · ~5–10 min drive across the Bong Bridge from Duluth
EMT-Basic Advanced EMT Paramedic

The only school in this guide that offers every level of EMS training under one roof — and the closest such option to Duluth. Northwood Tech (formerly WITC) is a Wisconsin technical college, but Minnesota residents pay the same in-state-equivalent tuition rate via WI/MN reciprocity. Paramedic Technician (Associate Degree, AAS)

Layers general-education credits on top of the EMT-Paramedic technical diploma to reach a 67-credit AAS, available 2 years part-time. Available at all four campuses including Superior. Per Northwood Tech's 2022–23 graduate survey, 83% of paramedic technician graduates were employed within six months. Total estimated cost approaches $11,000 in tuition before books and supplies.

Typical weekly load

  • EMT — typically two evenings per week of lecture/lab (~8–10 hours classroom contact) plus required clinical hours; expect 10–15 total hours per week.
  • Paramedic — two evenings of online lecture (~6–8 hrs) + every-other-weekend skills (~12–16 hrs/lab weekend) + clinical & field internship hours that scale up dramatically in months 6–16. Effectively a heavy part-time commitment; the program is intentionally evening/weekend-built so students can keep working.
Why this is the strongest "all-in" option — Wisconsin's technical-college pricing is among the lowest in the region, the Superior campus is a 10-minute drive from downtown Duluth, and you can ladder EMT → AEMT → Paramedic Technician → Bachelor's (transfer to UW-Stout) all through a single institution. Financial aid available for the AAS; potential WAI-2 grant support for short-term programs.

Minnesota North College

Mesabi Range Eveleth · Vermilion (Ely) · Itasca (Grand Rapids) · Hibbing — 50–80 miles north of Duluth
EMT-Basic No Paramedic

Worth knowing about if you live north of Duluth on the Iron Range, or if you're already commuting to one of these towns for work. Minnesota North College is the merged identity of five former community colleges (Hibbing, Itasca, Mesabi Range, Rainy River, Vermilion). It runs an 8-credit EMT course at multiple campuses, and offers an EMT bridge for already-certified Emergency Medical Responders at the Vermilion (Ely) campus.

The Pathway

From bystander to paramedic — a Duluth-area roadmap.

EMS credentials stack. You can stop at any rung. But each rung up requires the one beneath it to be current and in good standing.

Step One · ~16 weeks

EMT-Basic

Pick from LSC, Mayo Ambulance, Northwood Tech (Superior), or a Minnesota North campus. Cost: $1,100–$2,200. Pass the NREMT cognitive and psychomotor exams. Apply for your MN or WI state EMT license. You can now work on an ambulance.

Optional · ~232 hours

Advanced EMT

Only available locally through Northwood Tech (typically Rice Lake / New Richmond). Adds IV access, medication administration, and expanded patient assessment. Useful in Wisconsin services; less common in Minnesota.

Step Two · 16–20 months

Paramedic

Two real options: Mayo Clinic (Duluth satellite — 20 months, $12,600, August start, January–April application window) or Northwood Tech (Superior-accessible via online lectures, 16 months, ~$5,000, evening & weekend schedule). Both require a current EMT license to start.

Beyond · Bachelor's

Paramedic AAS & B.S.

Northwood Tech offers a 67-credit Paramedic Technician AAS that includes the EMT-Paramedic diploma plus general education. Articulation agreements with UW-Stout (B.S. in Career, Technical Education & Training, 100% online) for those who want to teach or move into EMS leadership.

Before You Apply Anywhere

The universal pre-flight checklist.

Every program in this guide expects roughly the same baseline. Hit these and you'll move through admissions without surprises.

For EMT-Basic

  • ▸ Age 16–17 minimum to enroll (varies by school); 18 to certify
  • ▸ HS diploma not always required at enrollment, but is for licensure
  • ▸ Background check — MN uses NetStudy / EMSRB; WI uses Caregiver Background Check
  • ▸ Felony-free record required for national certification
  • ▸ BLS CPR certification — usually scheduled within the program
  • ▸ Basic physical capability — patient lifting, sustained activity

For Paramedic

  • ▸ HS diploma or GED — required at application
  • ▸ Age 18+ by program start
  • ▸ Current NREMT EMT-Basic certification — non-negotiable
  • ▸ State EMT license (MN EMSRB or WI DHS) — also non-negotiable
  • ▸ AHA BLS provider card (current)
  • ▸ Valid driver's license
  • ▸ U.S. citizen / permanent resident (Mayo specifically — no visa sponsorship)
  • ▸ One college-level course for first-time college students (Medical Terminology recommended)

Money Help

  • ▸ MN Paramedic Scholarship — $5,000/year, renewable once. Open to MN residents enrolled in eligible MN paramedic programs (Mayo qualifies). Apply through the MN Office of Higher Education.
  • ▸ FAFSA / federal aid — applies to LSC, Northwood Tech AAS programs, MN North
  • ▸ WAI-2 grant — Northwest Wisconsin Workforce Investment Board funding for short-term tech-college programs
  • ▸ Mayo employee tuition assistance — institutional support for Mayo Clinic employees pursuing the Paramedic program
  • ▸ Mayo's EMT-in-Training program — paid as employee; tuition, books, and laptop covered
A note on licensure

Whichever school you pick in this region, the certification process is the same: complete the course → pass the National Registry of EMTs (NREMT) cognitive exam → pass the NREMT psychomotor (skills) exam → apply for your state license through the Minnesota EMSRB (mn.gov/oems) or the Wisconsin Department of Health Services EMS Section. National Registry status alone does not let you work — you need state licensure on top.

Wisconsin and Minnesota offer reasonable reciprocity, so an EMT trained at Northwood Tech can work in Duluth, and an LSC graduate can work in Superior — but each transition requires a separate state license application.
